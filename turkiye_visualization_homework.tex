\section{Introduction}

This report presents the design process and implementation for a visualization homework project based on Turkish population and socio-economic data. The study focuses on the years 2019 to 2024 and investigates whether changes in the populations of selected major cities move together with national indicators such as unemployment rate, GDP per capita, and inflation.

The report combines hand-drawn sketches, design rationale, and one coded visualization. Some sections intentionally contain placeholders so that additional handwritten images, screenshots, reflections, and course-specific terminology can be inserted later.

\textbf{Main research question:}

What relationship exists between population growth in major Turkish cities and national socio-economic indicators such as unemployment rate, GDP per capita, and inflation between 2019 and 2024?

\textbf{Visualization type:} Multi-line comparison chart with dual-axis scaling

\textbf{Goal:}  
The goal of this visualization is to compare temporal trends between population growth in major Turkish cities and national socio-economic indicators such as unemployment rate, GDP per capita, and inflation. By aligning all variables along the same timeline, the visualization helps reveal whether changes in economic conditions coincide with changes in population trends.

\textbf{Design rationale:}

This visualization uses a multi-line chart because it is effective for showing temporal patterns and comparing multiple variables over time. The horizontal axis encodes the time dimension (2019–2024), while vertical position encodes the magnitude of each variable. Different colors represent different indicators, allowing viewers to easily distinguish the trends. Lines and connected points act as visual marks that emphasize direction and slope, making increases or decreases clearly visible. A secondary axis is used for the population index so that variables with different scales can still be compared within the same view. This encoding enables quick identification of correlations, divergences, and turning points between demographic and economic indicators.

\textbf{Where this could be useful:}

This visualization could be useful for policymakers, economists, or researchers interested in understanding how demographic changes in major cities relate to broader economic conditions. It can also support exploratory analysis in educational or analytical settings.

\textbf{Strengths:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Clearly communicates trends and changes over time.
    \item Enables direct comparison between multiple indicators.
    \item Makes it easier to visually detect possible correlations between variables.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Weaknesses:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Dual-axis charts can sometimes be misinterpreted if scales are not carefully labeled.
    \item Too many variables could reduce readability and visual clarity.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Visualization type:} Radial (polar) multi-variable comparison chart

\textbf{Goal:}  
The goal of this visualization is to represent the evolution of socio-economic indicators and urban population trends in a circular timeline. By arranging the years around a radial axis, the visualization allows viewers to observe how different indicators change relative to each other across time.

\textbf{Design rationale:}

This visualization uses a radial layout to emphasize the cyclical and comparative nature of temporal data. Each year is encoded as an angular segment around the circle, while the magnitude of each indicator is represented by radial distance from the center. Different indicators are encoded using color and line shapes, allowing viewers to distinguish between unemployment rate, inflation, GDP per capita, and average population. The radial bars represent the population index, while layered polygonal lines represent economic indicators. This design uses spatial position and color as visual channels to encode quantitative differences and enables comparison of patterns across years.

\textbf{Where this could be useful:}

This type of visualization can be useful in exploratory analysis or presentations where the goal is to highlight patterns or relationships between multiple indicators in a compact visual form. It may also help audiences quickly compare different years and identify unusual economic changes.

\textbf{Strengths:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Provides an intuitive overview of multiple variables in a single compact layout.
    \item Makes it easier to compare the relative magnitude of indicators across years.
    \item Visually distinctive representation that highlights patterns and spikes.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Weaknesses:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Radial charts can be harder to interpret precisely compared to linear charts.
    \item Comparing exact values across different angles may be less intuitive for viewers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Task 3: Implemented Visualization}

For the implementation stage, the second visualization concept (the radial socio-economic comparison visualization) was selected and implemented as an interactive web-based visualization. The implementation was developed as a standalone HTML file named \texttt{sketch2.html}, which contains embedded JavaScript using the D3.js visualization library.

\subsection{Chosen Visualization}

The radial multi-indicator visualization (Sketch 2) was selected for implementation because it allows multiple variables to be represented in a single integrated view while maintaining a clear temporal structure. The circular layout enables each year between 2019 and 2024 to be positioned around a central axis, making it easier to compare how different indicators evolve over time.

In addition, the radial structure makes it possible to visually combine population growth and national socio-economic indicators within the same graphical space. This design helps reveal possible relationships between demographic change and economic conditions.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Technology used:} HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and the D3.js (v7) visualization library.
    
    \item \textbf{File structure:} The visualization is implemented as a single standalone file called \texttt{sketch2.html}. All styling, data, and JavaScript code are embedded within this file, allowing it to be opened directly in a web browser without additional dependencies.
    
    \item \textbf{Interaction techniques used:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Hover tooltips that display numerical values for each year.
        \item Interactive highlighting of indicators through the legend.
        \item Mouseover effects that emphasize selected data layers.
        \item Interactive wedges that reveal detailed information about each year.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Visual encoding:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Years (2019–2024) are encoded as angular positions around the circle.
        \item Population values are represented by radial wedge size.
        \item GDP per capita, inflation, and unemployment are encoded as layered radial polygons.
        \item Color is used to differentiate indicators (green for GDP, orange for inflation, blue for unemployment).
        \item Radial distance from the center encodes the magnitude of each variable.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implementation Reflection}

The implemented visualization successfully integrates multiple socio-economic indicators and population data into a single interactive representation. The radial design makes it possible to observe how economic indicators and population trends evolve over time within the same visual structure.

Interactive elements such as hover tooltips and legend highlighting improve usability by allowing users to inspect specific values and focus on individual indicators. This helps the visualization answer the research question by making temporal patterns and potential relationships between demographic and economic changes easier to observe.

However, while the radial format is visually engaging and effective for pattern recognition, it may be less precise for detailed numerical comparison compared to traditional line charts. Future improvements could include additional interactive controls, filtering options, or alternative views to support more precise analytical exploration.

\section{Discussion}

The visualizations created in this project reveal several patterns regarding the relationship between urban population trends and national socio-economic indicators in Türkiye between 2019 and 2024. One noticeable observation is that most of the selected cities show relatively steady population growth over the studied period. Cities such as Ankara, İzmir, and Muğla display gradual increases, while İstanbul shows slight fluctuations but generally remains stable at a high population level.

When these population patterns are compared with national economic indicators, some interesting relationships emerge. During the same period, unemployment rates show a consistent downward trend, while GDP per capita increases significantly after 2021. Inflation, on the other hand, experiences a sharp increase between 2021 and 2023 before declining slightly in 2024. These contrasting trends suggest that while economic indicators fluctuate strongly, urban population changes tend to evolve more gradually.

From the visualizations, population growth does not appear to react immediately to short-term economic shocks such as inflation spikes. Instead, demographic changes seem to follow longer-term structural trends. However, the overall improvement in GDP per capita and decreasing unemployment may still contribute to gradual population growth in major urban centers over time. The visualizations therefore help highlight how demographic patterns and economic indicators evolve simultaneously but at different temporal scales.

\section{Conclusion}

This homework explored the relationship between urban population change and national socio-economic indicators in Türkiye between 2019 and 2024. The project involved three stages: exploratory sketching, concept generation, and coded implementation. Through these stages, the report aimed to move from raw tabular values toward visual forms that support comparison, trend analysis, and interpretation.

Overall, the visualizations suggest that population changes in major Turkish cities tend to follow stable and gradual trends, while national socio-economic indicators such as inflation, unemployment, and GDP per capita fluctuate more rapidly. The visualization process made it easier to observe these differences in temporal behavior and to explore potential relationships between demographic and economic dynamics. This demonstrates how visualization techniques can help transform complex numerical datasets into interpretable visual insights.
